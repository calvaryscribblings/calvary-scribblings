You Didn't Ask

By Tricia Ajax · Mar 4, 2026
← Back to Short Stories

Bella was still on the phone with him when she lit the last candle.

The room smelled of vanilla and something floral she couldn't name. She'd chosen the scents carefully, the way she chose everything these days — with intention, without urgency. She was learning to want things slowly.

'I'm almost done,' she told Daniel, cradling the phone between her ear and shoulder while she shook out the match. 'Just give me a few minutes.'

'Take your time.' His voice was easy, unhurried. She liked that about him.

The bath was full. The water was the right temperature — hot enough to sting at first, then surrender to. She'd added salts and a few drops of oil she'd bought on a whim six months ago and never opened. Tonight felt like the right occasion, though she couldn't say exactly why.

She told Daniel she'd call him back and set the phone face-down on the tiles.

Lowering herself into the water, she exhaled.

She thought, as she often did in quiet moments, about how strange it was to feel fine. Not happy in any particular direction — not pointed toward anything — just fine. Settled. She'd spent so many years confusing turbulence for aliveness that stillness had once frightened her. Now she understood: stillness was not emptiness. It was the thing underneath, the ground you stood on when everything else was stripped away.

She was thirty-one. She lived alone. She had good work, a few close friends, and one plant she had not yet killed. These were not small things.

She stayed in the bath until the candle had burned a quarter of the way down. Then she dried off, pulled on the robe she'd had since university and refused to replace, and padded into the kitchen to make tea.

While the kettle boiled, she picked up her phone. There was a message from her mother — a photo of the garden, captioned: Your father says the tomatoes are early this year. She smiled and typed back a heart.

There was nothing from Emeka.

She noticed this the way you notice the absence of a sound you'd long stopped expecting — not with pain, exactly, but with a kind of recognition. It had been eight months since they'd spoken. She didn't miss him the way she once had, that desperate, reaching kind of missing. What remained was quieter: the occasional wish that things had been different, that they had both been different, earlier, when it might have mattered.

But she hadn't been ready then. And he hadn't known how to wait.

She made her tea — too strong, as always — and took it to the window seat. The street below was quiet. A man walked a dog. A light was on in the flat across the road. Somewhere, someone was living their life, and it had nothing to do with her, and that was perfectly fine.

She thought about calling Daniel back. She thought about reading. She thought about nothing in particular, the thoughts drifting through like weather.

In the end, she just sat there, holding her mug, watching the street.

She hadn't asked for this life — this specific configuration of solitude and small pleasures and hard-won equilibrium. But she hadn't asked for the other things either, the ones that had broken her open and left her to figure out what she was made of.

Turns out: something that holds.

The candle in the bathroom burned on, slow and steady, long after she'd forgotten about it.
