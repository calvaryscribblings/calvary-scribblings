The alarm went off at five.

Nkechi didn't reach for the snooze button. She never did anymore — not since the morning she missed it, and missed everything else that followed.

She sat up slowly, swinging her feet to the cold floor the way her mother taught her. *Both feet, all at once. Don't negotiate with yourself before the sun comes up.*

The curtains were thin. Lagos light leaked through them like something impatient, like something that had places to be. She let it fall across her hands while she prayed — not on her knees, not with any formal words, just the way a person holds a conversation they've been putting off.

The kettle clicked on. She stood at the window.

Below, the street was already alive: a bread seller balancing a tray on her head with the carelessness of someone who'd been doing it since before she could remember. A young man hosing down a car that didn't belong to him. A child in a school uniform too big for her body, walking with the gravity of someone carrying important news.

*They are already at it*, she thought. *All of them.*

She poured her tea. Sat at the small table by the door. Opened her notebook — not her phone, not first thing — and wrote three sentences about whatever was true right now.

*The bread seller's tray catches the light. My tea is too hot. I am still here.*

That last one always surprised her. Not in a dark way. In the way of a gift you don't expect — the simple, stubborn fact of continuity. Of having arrived, again, at another morning.

She finished her tea. Stood. Got dressed with the particular care of someone who believed that how you put yourself together said something, even if no one was watching — *especially* if no one was watching.

At five forty-seven, she opened the front door.

The heat hit her like a reminder.

She smiled at it — *old friend* — and stepped out to meet the day.
